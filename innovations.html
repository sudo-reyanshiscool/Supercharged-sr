04 · Chapter

four ideas
that earn their grams.

Every gram of mass needs to justify itself in milliseconds. These four innovations did, and they're why Dasher cleared 1.330 s on the Denford track before Singapore.

Innov · 01

magnetic
nosecone.

Five-by-two-millimetre notches in body and nose, joined by an N52 neodymium magnet. Snap-swap the nose during testing. No superglue. No aero compromise. The magnet holds the seam tighter than glue ever did and lets us iterate ten noses in an afternoon.

The seam is invisible to the air. The convenience is invisible to the judges. Both serve us.

Snap-swapField-serviceable
Innov · 02

split bearing
system.

Dual bearings per wheel hold the axle on a true straight line. Less wobble, less lateral deviation, more of the CO₂ thrust converting into actual forward motion. Our race model says wheel friction is 21.4 % of total drag — so the wheels deserved an architecture, not a part.

The result: Boca-grade ceramic-hybrid bearings on a self-aligned axle. Smooth, repeatable, fast.

21.4 %of total drag · saved
Innov · 03

aerofins
+ halo lip.

Small fins above the halo and a vertical lip below it. Dirty incoming air becomes straight streamlines that get pushed cleanly over and around the cockpit. The principle is borrowed from modern F1 — the halo is a known disruptor; we treat it as a vortex generator instead.

Subtle to the eye. Decisive in the data.

InspiredModern F1 halo geometry
Innov · 04

mathematical
race model.

900+ lines of Python integrating thrust curves, wheel friction, drag, tether yaw and guide-base height. Predicts race time, side displacement and yaw before a chip is cut. Three thousand Monte Carlo runs per design iteration tells us what's robust and what's lucky.

It's how a school team behaves like a Formula team. Decisions made on numbers, not on hope.
